Does Uranus have rings?more_vert

Uranus' Rings: NASA

Yes, but not as flashy as Saturn.close

Uranus has two sets of rings. The nine inner rings are narrow and dark grey making them less visible and not as prominent as Saturn's rings. The outer two rings, however, are much easier to see than those closer to the surface of the icy Gas Giant. The innermost ring of the two outer rings is a dusty reddish color, and the outermost ring is blue like Saturn's E-ring. Each of Uranus' rings are also named. Starting with the ring closest to Uranus and moving outward, the rings are called Zeta, 6, 5, 4, Alpha, Beta, Eta, Gamma, Delta, Lambda, Epsilon, Nu and Mu.

What's different about Uranus' orbit?more_vert

Uranus Orbit: NASA

It appears to roll on its side.close

Uranus is the only planet in our star system whose equator is nearly at a right angle to its orbit. It tilts at just shy of 98 degrees as opposed to Earth's 23.5 degree tilt. This, coupled with its rings, gives the appearance that Uranus is orbiting on its side, like a ball rolling around the sun. It is unknown exactly why the tilt is so severe, but it is thought that the tilt is a result of an ancient collision with an Earth sized object. As a result of this unique tilt, Uranus has the most extreme seasons in the solar system. For a quarter of each Uranian year (about 21 Earth years), the sun shines directly over each of the planet's poles leaving the opposite pole in icy darkness for the duration.
