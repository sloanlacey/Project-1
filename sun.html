How hot is the sun?more_vert

The Sun: NASA

It's really, really hot.close

Just how hot the sun actually is depends on if we are measuring the heat from its core or at the surface. The sun's surface, also known as the photosphere, is a toasty 10,000 degrees Fahrenheit which is roughly 5 times hotter than any lava on Earth. Its core, however, is a blistering 27 million degrees Fahrenheit. That's really hot!

The Sun
  • Mass: 1.989 x 10^30 kg
  • Rotational Period: the sun rotates on its axis once every 27 days, and it orbits the Milky Way galaxy once every 250 million years
  • Average distance from Earth: 93 million miles
What type of star is the sun?more_vert

Sun Overview: NASA

A yellow dwarf.close

While the sun is the largest object in our solar system comprising about 99.8% of our system's total mass, it is considered a relatively small star compared to some of the other types in the universe. Stars are classified in several ways including the Harvard Classification which assigns a single letter based on the photospheric (surface) temperature of the star, or the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram which classifies stars on a scatter plot based on their luminosity vs. their effective temperature. The sun is the center of our universe and is an extremely important part of how life survives on planet Earth but, as we can see from various diagrams, the sun pales in comparison to stars further out in the universe.
